Old West Pong – Blitz 2D
A good while ago, my family had a game called SuperPong95 (for Windows 95) on our computer. It had six modes, of which Old West Pong was the most fun. Last year, I went looking for it online because I wanted to play it again. However, to my dismay, I couldn't find it anywhere. I wasn't content to not play it though, so I decided to recreate it. The finished product can be found here on SourceForge: Old West Pong

Mapping the School – Blitz 3D
My high school (NCSSM) split its year into trimesters, with a so-called "miniterm" in between the winter and spring trimesters that lasted for 10 days. Students could do just about anything they wanted as long as it involved learning something non-trivial. Senior year, I chose to do this project. I envisioned creating something like Google Maps, but 3D and for the school campus. Over the span of 3 days, I wrote about 18 kB of code that allowed me to create a node network where I could create new nodes, link them together, move them around, and so on. Furthermore, it also has lists of rooms on the side that can be scrolled through to select rooms, and the shortest path is highlighted in yellow. Here are two screenshots below:

Triangular Pegs Game Solver – Python
This one is probably among the most advanced and significant of my Python programs. I have included it here mainly to show that I do have significant Python experience. The IDE I use for Blitz 2D/3D is simply much more conducive to large projects, which is why almost all of these projects are in that language. That said, this one demonstrates what I now know to be knowledge of the basics of artificial intelligence. In Pegs-type games, the only move is to jump one peg over another in a straight line, removing the one that was jumped over. It is often seen at places like Cracker Barrel and other establishments that have old, old board games. My program was focused on the triangular version (pictured below), and I wrote it in order to find a solution path that would place the final peg smack dab in the middle. As it turned out, my many failed attempts at finding that solution myself were not actually failures, because there wasn't such a solution. There was, however, a solution for placing the final peg in the middle of the bottom side, and my program found two paths which were mirrors of each other.

ArbyChess – Blitz 2D
This is a chess engine with the goal of allowing very customizable boards and pieces. For instance, this engine will allow the board to wrap around horizontally, or for each side to connect to the one clockwise from it, or a great many other configurations. At the time of writing, it is very close to being able to play a full game of chess. At the moment, in addition to traditional chess, it can run Hex-a-Pawn and hexagonal chess (specifically, Gliński's variant). The first and third are shown below. For more details, click here.
